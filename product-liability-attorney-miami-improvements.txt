[2026-07-16] FIX #1 - "Product Warranty Violations" definition was legally wrong
             File   : Views/ServicePage/Content/injury-law/
                      product-liability-attorney-miami/En/article.cshtml
             Status : APPLIED TO WORKING TREE - NOT YET REVIEWED BY AN ATTORNEY
                      Do not publish until Gallardo signs off. See RISK below.

  WHAT WAS WRONG
    The English page defined a warranty violation as a failure to warn:
      "These violations represent the inability of a company to provide a
       proper warning of the dangers of a product through normal or future
       misuse. It can also represent unclear or inaccurate instructions for
       the product."
    That is not warranty law. It describes failure-to-warn, which the page
    already covers as its own separate defect type two sections earlier. The
    page therefore listed the same concept twice and never explained warranty
    at all. A reader with a genuine breach-of-warranty claim would not have
    recognised it from this page.

  WHY IT MATTERED BEYOND ACCURACY
    Breach of warranty is a distinct cause of action from negligence and strict
    liability. Omitting it narrows the theories a reader thinks they have.

  WHAT IT SAYS NOW
    Defines the two warranties Florida actually recognises:
      - Express warranty: a specific claim the manufacturer or seller makes
        (advertising, packaging, manual, written guarantee) that becomes part
        of the basis of the sale.
      - Implied warranty: imposed automatically by law with no promise made -
        merchantability (fit for the ordinary purpose sold for) and fitness for
        a particular purpose (seller knows your use, you rely on their
        judgment).
      - Breach: product fails the promise or is not fit for ordinary use, and
        that failure causes injury or loss - a claim that can sit alongside
        negligence and strict liability.

  STATUTORY BASIS (for the reviewing attorney to confirm)
    Fla. Stat. 672.313 - express warranty: affirmation of fact or promise,
      description, or sample/model made part of the basis of the bargain. No
      formal words needed. Mere opinion or commendation does not create one.
    Fla. Stat. 672.314 - implied warranty of merchantability: implied where the
      seller is a merchant in goods of that kind; goods must pass without
      objection in the trade and be fit for ordinary purposes.
    Fla. Stat. 672.315 - implied warranty of fitness for a particular purpose:
      seller has reason to know the purpose and that the buyer relies on the
      seller's skill or judgment.

  RISK / OPEN QUESTIONS FOR THE ATTORNEY - two things I deliberately did NOT
  write into the page because they are judgment calls, not facts:
    1. PRIVITY. Florida requires privity of contract for implied warranty
       claims. A consumer who bought from a retailer generally cannot bring an
       implied warranty claim against the manufacturer. The new text says "you
       may have a breach of warranty claim" and does not mention this. That
       hedge may be too soft - Gallardo should decide whether to state the
       privity limit outright or drop the implied-warranty discussion for
       consumers entirely.
    2. MERCHANT LIMITATION. Merchantability is implied only when the seller is
       a merchant in goods of that kind. The new text omits this. A private
       one-off seller does not carry it.

  CORRECTION TO MY OWN EARLIER ADVICE
    When I reported this finding I said the Spanish page "correctly explains"
    Garantia Expresa and Garantia Implicita and that English should be ported
    from it. That was overstated. Spanish correctly IDENTIFIES the two
    categories but defines both loosely:
      - It calls express warranty "todas las etiquetas de advertencia o
        senalizaciones puestas en el producto" - warning labels are not an
        express warranty.
      - It calls implied warranty "toda promesa del fabricante... de que, si se
        usa segun lo previsto, no causara danos" - implied warranty is
        precisely the warranty that exists when NO promise is made; it is
        imposed by law, not promised by anyone.
    So the English fix above was written against the statute, NOT ported from
    the Spanish. THE SPANISH SECTION STILL NEEDS THE SAME FIX - it is wrong in
    a different, subtler way, and it is still live. Not yet actioned.

[2026-07-16] FIX #2 - causation section stated the law far too restrictively
             (audit item D)
             File   : Views/ServicePage/Content/injury-law/
                      product-liability-attorney-miami/En/article.cshtml
                      H3 "Proving that a defective product caused your injury"
             Status : APPLIED TO WORKING TREE - NOT YET REVIEWED BY AN ATTORNEY
                      Do not publish until Gallardo signs off.

  WHAT WAS WRONG
      "You can prove that you were injured as a result of a defective product
       by proving that the product was used as directed on the label and that
       the victim wasn't aware of its hazard before using it. This places
       responsibility on the manufacturer instead of the person who used it."
    Three problems, each of which narrows the reader's claim:
      1. "used as directed on the label" is not the test. Florida asks whether
         the product performed as safely as an ordinary consumer would expect
         when used as intended OR in a reasonably foreseeable manner. A
         foreseeable misuse still supports a claim.
      2. "wasn't aware of its hazard" is not a requirement. Awareness of risk
         does not bar recovery. The real defense is much narrower: unreasonable
         continued use AFTER discovering the actual defect and the danger.
      3. It omitted the actual causation elements entirely - but-for cause,
         proximate cause, and that the defect existed when the product left the
         defendant's control.
    Net effect: a reader who used a product slightly off-label, or who knew it
    carried some risk, would conclude they had no case. On an intake page that
    is the most expensive kind of error.

  WHAT IT SAYS NOW - three paragraphs:
    - Causation: but-for + foreseeable result, and the defect must have existed
      when the product left the manufacturer's control (not from a later
      modification or repair).
    - Intended vs foreseeable use: no need to have followed the label to the
      letter; foreseeable misuse does not end the claim; awareness of risk does
      not either; states the narrow real defense.
    - Comparative fault: damages reduced by the claimant's share, and the
      50%-bar (below).

  THE MATERIAL FACT THE PAGE WAS MISSING ALTOGETHER
    Florida is no longer a pure comparative negligence state. HB 837 created
    Fla. Stat. 768.81(6) on 24 Mar 2023: a party found MORE THAN 50% at fault
    for their own harm recovers NOTHING. And 768.81(1)(c) defines "negligence
    action" to expressly include strict liability, products liability and
    breach of warranty - so the 51% bar DOES reach every theory this page
    discusses. This is probably the single most important thing an injured
    Floridian needs to know in 2026, and the page did not mention it in either
    language. It is now in the EN page only.

  STATUTORY / CASE BASIS (for the reviewing attorney to confirm)
    Fla. Stat. 768.81(6)   - >50% at fault = no recovery (eff. 24 Mar 2023).
    Fla. Stat. 768.81(1)(c)- "negligence action" includes strict liability,
                             products liability, breach of warranty.
                             (Medical negligence under ch. 766 is excepted.)
    West v. Caterpillar Tractor Co., 336 So. 2d 80 (Fla. 1976) - Florida
                             adopted strict product liability.
    Elements: defect; defect existed when it left the defendant's control;
              defect was the actual and proximate cause of injury.
    Defenses: post-sale modification; unforeseeable misuse as the SOLE cause;
              unreasonable use after discovery of the defect and danger.

  RISK / OPEN QUESTIONS FOR THE ATTORNEY
    1. The new text says fault "usually" affects how much you recover rather
       than whether you can claim - then gives the 50% bar as the exception.
       Gallardo may prefer to lead with the bar rather than soften it.
    2. I did not address whether comparative fault principles apply identically
       to a pure strict-liability count as to a negligence count. 768.81(1)(c)
       says the section covers both, but the apportionment mechanics in a
       strict liability case are worth a lawyer's eye.
    3. The closing line advises speaking to a lawyer before giving a recorded
       statement to an insurer. True and useful, but it is advice - confirm it
       is the guidance the firm wants to give.

[2026-07-16] FIX #3 - opening section tied the right to sue to who BOUGHT the
             product (audit item C)
             File   : Views/ServicePage/Content/injury-law/
                      product-liability-attorney-miami/En/article.cshtml
                      H2 "What is Product Liability?"
             Status : APPLIED TO WORKING TREE - NOT YET REVIEWED BY AN ATTORNEY
                      Do not publish until Gallardo signs off. See RISK below.

  WHAT WAS WRONG
      "You can sue companies or manufacturers for this product if you or
       anyone you know bought it."
    The trigger for an injury claim is being INJURED, not purchasing. The
    sentence was wrong in both directions at once:
      - TOO BROAD: an acquaintance buying a product creates no claim for you.
        Read literally, a neighbour's purchase of a defective heater that never
        touched you would let you sue. It would not.
      - TOO NARROW: by framing the question around who bought it, it tells an
        injured non-purchaser they have nothing. Florida abolished privity for
        strict liability - a person hurt by a product they were given,
        borrowed, or merely stood near can bring a claim.
    Probable origin: a garbled version of the standard "you or a loved one
    [was injured]" formulation. It reads as a drafting accident, not a
    misunderstanding of the law.

  WHAT IT SAYS NOW
    States that the right to claim comes from being injured rather than from
    paying for the product; that you need not have bought it yourself to sue
    over an injury it caused; and that Florida protects anyone the manufacturer
    should reasonably have expected to be using the product or to be near it.
    Also replaced the opening clause "the act of holding a seller liable" with
    language covering the companies that make AND sell the product, since the
    rest of the paragraph already extends liability up the whole chain.

  BASIS (for the reviewing attorney to confirm)
    West v. Caterpillar Tractor Co., 336 So. 2d 80 (Fla. 1976) - adopted
      Restatement (2d) Torts 402A; abolished the privity requirement; extended
      protection beyond users and consumers to foreseeable bystanders within
      the danger zone of the product's use.
    Recovery elements per West: the product was in a defective condition
      unreasonably dangerous to foreseeable users OR BYSTANDERS at the time of
      sale, and that condition proximately caused the damages.

  RISK / OPEN QUESTION FOR THE ATTORNEY - ONE I INTRODUCED
    This fix and FIX #1 are in tension and a reader could combine them wrongly.
    Privity is abolished for STRICT LIABILITY and negligence (this section now
    says so), but Florida still REQUIRES privity for IMPLIED WARRANTY claims
    (flagged under FIX #1). A reader who takes "you do not need to have bought
    it" from this section and carries it into the warranty section could
    conclude they have an implied warranty claim against a manufacturer they
    never dealt with. They would not.
    I scoped the wording to "to sue over an injury it caused" - true of the
    tort theories - rather than stating it as a universal rule, but the two
    sections should be read together and reconciled by counsel. This is the
    cost of fixing accuracy issues one at a time; it is worth one pass over the
    whole page once the individual fixes are settled.
